## Project Overview
This repository contains the quantitative research and econometric modeling for the paper presented at the **Annual Student Scientific Conference (ASE Bucharest, 2026)**, where it was awarded **2nd Place** in the Economic Cybernetics section.

The study investigates the reconfiguration of macroeconomic priorities across 7 NATO Eastern Flank states (**Poland, Romania, Greece, Bulgaria, and the Baltics**) under the pressure of the new security paradigm. We analyze the "crowding-out" effect of military expenditure on **Gross Fixed Capital Formation (GFCF)** between 2004 and 2023.

## Methodology & Models
We utilized a **Panel Data** approach to capture both temporal and cross-sectional dynamics:
* **Model M1 (Linear/General):** Evaluates the baseline trade-off between defense, social spending, and investment.
* **Model M2 (Log-Log/Asymmetric):** Explores the elasticity of GFCF relative to specialized NATO equipment expenditure, splitting countries into "Producers" (e.g., Poland) and "Importers" (e.g., Romania).
* **Statistical Rigor:** * **Hausman Test:** Used to determine the efficiency of Random Effects (RE) vs. Fixed Effects (FE).
    * **Im-Pesaran-Shin (IPS):** Panel unit root tests for stationarity.
    * **Cointegration:** Analysis of long-term equilibrium between defense spending and capital growth.

## Key Findings
* **Producer Advantage:** In states with domestic defense industries (Poland, Greece), military equipment acquisition shows a positive impact on internal investment ($\beta \approx 0.199, p < 0.05$).
* **Import Leakage:** In "importer" states (Romania, Bulgaria), defense spending acts as a fiscal "leakage" through imports, showing a neutral effect on domestic capital formation.
* **The Social Paradox:** Surprisingly, the strongest crowding-out effect on productive investment stems from social expenditures ($\beta = -1.45, p < 0.01$), rather than military ones.
